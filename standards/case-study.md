# Case Study and Ethnography Standard
Source: ACM SIGSOFT Empirical Standards, CC0.

## Essential Attributes
- Justifies the selection of the case(s) or site(s) studied
- Describes the site(s) in rich detail
- The site is a real (physical or virtual) place, not a toy example
- Reports the type of case study:
  descriptive | emancipatory | evaluative | explanatory | exploratory | historical | revelatory
- Describes data sources (participant demographics, work roles)
- Defines unit(s) of analysis or observation
- Presents a clear chain of evidence from observations to findings

## Desirable Attributes
- Provides supplementary materials (interview guides, coding schemes, decision rules)
- Triangulates across data sources, informants, or researchers
- Cross-checks interviewee statements against direct observation or archival records
- Uses participant observation or direct observation and integrates it into results
- Validates results using member checking, dialogical interviewing, or research audits
- Describes external events that may have affected the case
- Uses quotations to illustrate findings (not as the only representation of a finding)
- EITHER evaluates an a priori theory using deductive coding
  OR synthesizes results into a new theory using inductive coding
- Researchers reflect on their own possible biases

## General Quality Criteria
Credibility, multivocality, reflexivity, rigor, transferability.
Quantitative criteria (replicability, generalizability, objectivity) typically do NOT apply.

## Invalid Criticisms
- Does not present quantitative data
- Sample of one; findings not generalizable
  (the point of a case study is to study one thing deeply, not generalize to a population)
- Lack of internal validity
  (only applies to explanatory case studies seeking to establish causality)
- Lack of reproducibility or data disclosure
  (qualitative data are often confidential)
- Insufficient number or length of interviews
  (what matters is that findings are credible and description is deep and rich)
